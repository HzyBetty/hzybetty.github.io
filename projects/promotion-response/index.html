The problem

Retail promotions are expensive — sending an offer to a customer who wouldn't respond wastes budget and risks promotion fatigue. The goal is to estimate the probability that a given customer responds to a specific promotion in April 2013, using only their purchase history before the promotion date.

This is formulated as binary classification: active = 1 (responded) vs active = 0 (did not respond), evaluated on ROC AUC to account for the natural class imbalance in promotional response data.

Methodology

The pipeline has three stages. The most important design constraint throughout is temporal leakage prevention — every feature is computed using only transactions that occurred strictly before each promotion date.

Feature engineering

Over 50 features were engineered across six behavioural dimensions. High-cardinality identifiers (customer, store, brand, category) were encoded with Bayesian-smoothed target encoding to prevent overfitting on rare categories.

RFM behaviour
  • Recency (overall, brand, category)
  • Transaction frequency & counts
  • Total & average spend
  • Recent 30-day purchase count
  • Customer tenure in days
Brand & category affinity
  • Brand loyalty score
  • Brand recency & spend
  • Category purchase history
  • Brand share within category
  • Purchased category before (flag)
Store context
  • Store avg. quantity & spend
  • Store transaction volume
  • Customer–store affinity ratio
  • Normalised behavioural ratios
Price sensitivity
  • Promo value vs customer avg.
  • Promo qty vs customer avg. qty
  • Amount coefficient of variation
  • Promo price per unit
Temporal signals
  • Day of week, month
  • Weekend indicator
  • Weekend shopper flag
  • Day-of-week purchase patterns
Competitive pressure
  • Competing promos (30-day window)
  • Same store & category activity
  • Manufacturer-level recency
  • Target-encoded promo ID

Model & results

LightGBM was chosen over neural networks given the tabular structure and the overfitting observed even with gradient boosting. Hyperparameters were tuned via grid search with early stopping (50 rounds) on a stratified 80/20 validation split.

What drives promotion response?

Feature importance from the tuned LightGBM model reveals that overall engagement intensity dominates — customers who buy frequently and spend more are the most likely responders. Recency and brand loyalty are the next strongest signals. Manufacturer-level variables and calendar indicators contributed the least.

Future directions

Several avenues could improve both performance and business applicability:

  • Rolling temporal features — trend and seasonality signals over 60/90-day windows
  • Customer cohort segmentation — separate models per RFM tier may outperform a single global model
  • Ensemble stacking — combining LightGBM with a calibrated logistic regression for better probability estimates
  • SHAP-based feature selection — use SHAP importance rather than split counts for more stable pruning
  • Precision–recall optimisation — tune the decision threshold if false positive and false negative costs differ
